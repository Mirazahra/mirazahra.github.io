The Truth About Kibble: What’s Really in Your Dog’s Bowl

Kibble is the go-to food for millions of pet owners, marketed as “complete and balanced” by both manufacturers and veterinarians. But is kibble truly the nutritional safeguard it claims to be? A closer look reveals alarming truths about the kibble industry, its ingredients, and its impact on our pets’ health.

The Ingredients: Dead, Diseased, and Deceptive

Most kibble is made from meat and meat by-products sourced from dead, diseased, or dying animals deemed unfit for human consumption. These scraps are mixed with starches and carbohydrates that dogs are not biologically designed to digest, such as rice, legumes, corn, and oatmeal. To compensate for the nutrient loss during processing, manufacturers add synthetic vitamin and mineral packs. But how accurate are these mixes? And are they truly bioavailable to your dog? Unfortunately, the answers are murky at best.

The Manufacturing Process: High Heat, Low Nutrition

Kibble is made through extrusion—a process that subjects the ingredients to extremely high temperatures multiple times. This cooking method destroys most of the natural nutrients, enzymes, and beneficial properties of the raw ingredients, leaving behind a product that is essentially dead. The result is a shelf-stable food that prioritizes convenience over quality.

The Problem with “Complete and Balanced” Claims

The phrase “complete and balanced” is a marketing tool rather than a guarantee of optimal nutrition. Pet food companies rely on general guidelines that fail to consider individual dogs' unique needs. A one-size-fits-all formula assumes that all dogs—regardless of age, breed, size, or health status—require the same nutrients in the same proportions. This approach ignores the reality that nutrition should be as individual as the dog itself.
